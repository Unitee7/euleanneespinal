The Situation

Calla & Co. had been operating for about 18 months when the founders reached out. They had real momentum — several events executed well, strong word-of-mouth referrals, and an aesthetic point of view that clients responded to immediately. By most measures, the business was working.

But they had been operating entirely reactively. They said yes to whatever came in, priced each engagement based on a rough feeling, and treated every event as a one-off rather than an instance of a defined service model. Meanwhile, a growing list of things they wanted to build — a corporate events tier, portfolio shoots, a vendor partnership program, a proper inquiry process — kept getting talked about and kept staying undone.

The problem wasn't motivation or skill. It was the absence of a structure for deciding which idea to move on, in what order, and with what they actually had available. Every week felt like starting over.

"We had the drive and we had the clients. What we didn't have was a plan. Every week we'd talk about what we wanted to build and every week nothing moved."

The Work

The engagement started with a structured brain dump — getting every idea, concern, goal, and distraction out of both founders' heads and into a shared document where it could actually be looked at. From there, we worked through a prioritization process that separated what needed to happen now from what could wait, and what depended on something else being built first.

I

Idea Inventory & Prioritization

Every active idea was captured, then sorted by revenue impact, time required, and sequencing dependencies. Several ideas that felt urgent turned out to require foundational work that hadn't happened yet — which is exactly why they kept stalling.

II

Offer Clarity + Service Tier Design

The service model was undefined enough that they were pricing differently for similar events and struggling to explain what they offered to prospective clients. We defined three tiers with distinct scope, deliverables, and pricing logic — so the answer to "what do you offer?" was no longer improvised.

III

90-Day Execution Plan

A phased plan covering the first 90 days: formalizing the inquiry and onboarding process, and building a portfolio through two planned styled shoots. Sequenced weekly, with defined milestones and success indicators so the founders could tell whether things were moving.

IV

Core Business Documentation

A business overview, service description document, and client inquiry workflow — foundational documents the business had never had in written form. Built to be usable by both founders and shareable with prospective clients.

V

Follow-Through System

A biweekly check-in rhythm and simple progress tracker so the founders could see what was moving between sessions — and so new ideas had a place to go without derailing what was already in progress.

The Service Tiers We Defined

One of the most immediate impacts of the engagement was giving the founders language for what they already offered. These three tiers were built from how they already worked — not invented from scratch.

Tier 01
Day-of Coordination

For clients who have planned their own event and need a professional to execute it. Defined scope, timeline management, and vendor coordination on the day itself.

Tier 02
Partial Planning

For clients who want guidance on key decisions — venue, vendors, budget — without full-service engagement. A defined set of touchpoints and deliverables across the planning period.

Tier 03
Full-Service Coordination

End-to-end event management from concept through execution. Full vendor management, design direction support, budget tracking, and day-of presence.

Outcome

At the 30-day check-in, both founders described the primary change as knowing where to put their energy on any given day. The inquiry workflow had been implemented and was already creating a more consistent client experience. The first styled shoot had been planned and was on the calendar. Two prospective clients who had been in conversation for weeks both booked — the founders credited being able to clearly explain what they were getting.

More than any specific document, what the engagement produced was a shared direction. A plan both founders had helped build and both understood — which meant it was actually being followed.
