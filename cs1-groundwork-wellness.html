From Scattered Operations

Groundwork Wellness — Tampa, FL

The Situation

Groundwork Wellness had been operating for about two years when the owner reached out. Classes were full, trainers were booked, and client retention was strong. By most outward measures, the studio was doing well.

But behind the scenes, things were harder than they needed to be. Scheduling was managed across three tools that didn't communicate. New client onboarding happened differently depending on which trainer handled it. Membership renewals were tracked in a spreadsheet only the owner fully understood. When a trainer called out, coverage was figured out through individual texts — no protocol, no process, just the owner scrambling.

She wasn't looking to overhaul everything. She wanted to understand what was actually breaking down and fix it in a way her small team could realistically maintain without her being the single point of knowledge for all of it.

"We were busy, but busy wasn't the same as organized. I kept putting out the same fires every week and couldn't figure out why."

What I Found

The audit started with a structured intake session, followed by a review of how each operational area actually functioned day to day. I mapped the current state of four core workflows: new client onboarding, session scheduling and trainer coverage, membership management, and internal communication.

No single source of truth for scheduling

Three tools — a booking app, a group text thread, and a whiteboard — were all being used to manage trainer schedules. When information changed in one place, it rarely made it to the others.

Onboarding was person-dependent, not process-dependent

Each trainer handled new clients their own way. No standard intake process, no consistent handoff from front desk to trainer, no documentation of client history that anyone else could access.

Membership renewals had no trigger

Renewals were tracked manually in a spreadsheet, but nothing flagged upcoming expirations. The owner was catching them reactively — usually after a client had already stopped coming in.

No coverage protocol

When a trainer called out, the owner handled it through individual texts. No documented process, no backup contact order, no record of how previous situations had been resolved.

What I Built

The redesign focused on three things: consolidating where information lived, standardizing the processes that happened most often, and documenting everything clearly enough that the owner wasn't the only person who could run it.

Consolidated scheduling system with a defined source of truth and a clear update protocol — changes in one place get reflected everywhere.
New client onboarding SOP covering intake form collection, client history documentation, trainer handoff, and first-session confirmation. Consistent regardless of who handles it.
Membership renewal workflow with 30- and 7-day expiration flags and a short outreach email template — no longer dependent on the owner catching things manually.
Trainer coverage protocol: a documented escalation order and communication template for last-minute absences. The first time it was used after handoff, coverage was resolved without pulling the owner in.
Implementation guide and walkthrough session so the full team understood what changed and why.

Outcome

By the end of the engagement, the studio had a documented operational foundation it had never had before. The recurring fires the owner described — the same problems appearing week after week — dropped measurably in the first two weeks after implementation. The systems were designed to be maintained by the team, not just understood by her.

More than the specific deliverables, what this engagement produced was clarity: a clear picture of how the business actually operated, and a structure that matched the reality of a small team without adding unnecessary complexity.
