The Situation

The company had been managing 60+ residential units across four properties for several years. The operation ran well in practice — tenants were taken care of, maintenance got handled, rent came in. But there was no organized way to see how the portfolio was performing at any given moment.

Occupancy data lived in one spreadsheet. Maintenance requests were tracked in another. Lease expirations were in a calendar only one person could access. Revenue figures were in an accounting tool the other team member had never opened. When they needed an actual picture of how things were going — for a lender conversation, for year-end planning, for a hiring decision — someone had to spend hours pulling numbers from four places and assembling something that was already out of date by the time it was done.

What they needed wasn't a complex analytics platform. They needed a dashboard a two-person team could update in twenty minutes and read in five.

"We knew roughly how things were going. But 'roughly' wasn't good enough when someone needed an actual answer."

Identifying the Right Metrics

Before building anything, I worked with the owner to identify which numbers actually mattered for how the business made decisions. The question wasn't "what could we track" — it was "what do you look at when something needs your attention." We landed on six metrics that between them covered the full health of the portfolio.

1

Occupancy rate by property

The single most important number — and the one that had taken the longest to find before this engagement.

2

Days vacant per unit (rolling)

Flagging units sitting empty longer than average — an early signal of a pricing or listing problem.

3

Maintenance requests open vs. closed

Visibility into backlog and response time — two things that directly affect tenant retention.

4

Lease expirations in the next 60 days

A forward-looking view that gave the team time to act rather than react to turnover.

5

Monthly revenue vs. projected

Actual vs. expected, by property, so variance was visible at a glance without opening the accounting tool.

6

Tenant turnover rate (quarterly)

Tracked over time to surface whether retention was improving or declining — and prompt a conversation about why.

What I Built

The dashboard was built in Excel — chosen deliberately because it was the tool the team already knew. Adding a new platform would have created a maintenance problem, not solved one. I first audited and cleaned the existing data sources so numbers were consistent and comparable across all four properties.

The dashboard has three views: a weekly summary (the one they open every Monday), a property-by-property breakdown, and a 60-day forward-looking tab for lease and vacancy planning. Conditional formatting flags anything that needs attention — occupancy below threshold, maintenance backlog over a set number, leases expiring within 30 days — so the team doesn't have to read every row to know where to focus.

Cleaned data input template with consistent field names and validation rules across all four properties
Three-tab Excel dashboard: weekly summary, property breakdown, and 60-day planning view
Conditional formatting logic that surfaces issues without requiring manual review of every row
Maintenance guide documenting how to update each tab, where data comes from, and what each flag means
Walkthrough session with both team members to confirm independent maintenance capability

Outcome

The team went from spending several hours assembling a performance picture to having it ready every Monday morning in under twenty minutes. Within the first month of use, they caught two units trending toward extended vacancy early enough to adjust pricing before they sat empty. The lease expiration view surfaced three upcoming renewals that would have been missed entirely.

The less quantifiable outcome: both team members could now answer questions about how the portfolio was doing without going to each other or digging through separate tools. That information was visible, shared, and current.
